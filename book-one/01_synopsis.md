# Book I — One-Page Synopsis (working)

**Working title:** *Dr. Mineral and the Unsigned Discovery*
Derived from the locked decisions in `00_decisions-locked.md`. Canon source: `../docs/01_universe-bible-v1.md`. Middle-grade center, hopeful/cinematic, cooperative (non-revenge) arc, technology invisible.

---

Above an ordinary university, a humble campus squirrel collects acorns. Below it, that same squirrel is **Dr. Mineral** — founder and Chief Researcher of the **Hidden Laboratory**, a centuries-old underground research city run by intelligent animals, unknown to the humans walking overhead. A quiet tell carries us from the topside double life down into the wonder of the lab.

There Dr. Mineral takes on a new apprentice: a bright, fast-talking young **magpie** assigned to the **Contribution Registry** — the quiet district that records every contribution so that nothing, and no one, is ever forgotten. On her first real day, the apprentice notices something that shouldn't be possible: in the **Crystal Vault** rests a genuine, important discovery — and the Registry holds **no record of who contributed it.** In a lab built on the promise that *every contribution matters*, an unsigned discovery is more than a clerical gap. If one contribution can slip out of the record, any can — and the trust the whole lab runs on begins to wobble.

Dr. Mineral refuses both easy answers: he won't quietly claim the discovery, and he won't bury it. He does the thing the lab was *built* for — he **asks.** The mystery can't be cracked by one genius; it has to be solved the lab's way, by gathering many small observations and letting them add up. So the squirrel and the magpie travel the districts — **Central Research Hall**, the **Contribution Registry**, the **Crystal Vault**, the **Historical Archives** — collecting fragments of memory and record, each researcher's tiny recollection a puzzle piece. The invisible contribution network is never explained; it is *felt*, as trust: people remember, records hold, small things add up to large ones.

The truth, when it surfaces, is not theft and not a villain. The discovery was built by **many hands across a long time** — a contribution so distributed, and made by collaborators so uninterested in credit, that the lab's earliest record-keeping simply couldn't hold a thing with no single name on it. The "missing contributor" was, in a sense, *everyone*. Rather than crown one discoverer, Dr. Mineral and the apprentice **repair the lab itself**: they restore the lineage, honor the many, and improve how the Registry remembers distributed work — and it's the apprentice's brand-new, fresh-eyes observation that makes the fix possible. The smallest, newest contribution turns out to matter most. A real challenge, solved by cooperation and curiosity, with no one defeated.

In the restored record, one faint thread leads somewhere deeper than anyone had looked — an older question, a layer of the lab beneath the lab. The mystery closes; a larger one quietly opens, seeding Book II without a cliffhanger gimmick.
