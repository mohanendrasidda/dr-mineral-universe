# Chapter Nine — The Deep Past

*Book I — Dr. Mineral and the Unsigned Discovery · draft v1*
*Beat ② + ④ — the founding age revealed through the Historical Archives (lab as character). Answers: how the seed came down, and what "too many to name" meant to those who wrote it. Raises: a single celebrated name surfaces — is it the answer, or another lid? No tech named.*

---

The Historical Archives were the oldest rooms in our laboratory, and they smelled of the founding.

Tally noticed it the moment they came through the great low doorway — the dry sweet smell she had first met on the stair her very first morning, the smell of a great deal of kept paper, but here it was deeper, older, with something underneath it like the inside of an ancient tree. The Archives were not glassed and ticking like the Registry. They were warm and dim and close, a honeycomb of small reading-cells worn into the stone, and in every cell, on shelves that sagged with the patient weight of years, lay the journals. Not records of *that a thing was done.* The journals were where the founders had written what it had been *like.* The doubts. The cold winters. The arguments. The being afraid and going on anyway. The laboratory, remembering not its achievements but its heart.

"No one comes here much," said Vellum, and his voice was softer in this place, the way a voice goes soft in a room where someone is sleeping. "The Registry is for honoring. The Archives are for understanding. Most creatures would rather be honored than understood." He lowered his great bulk onto the worn stone with a sigh three hundred years deep. "Read to me, child. The founding shelves. Anything that speaks of the green, or the cold, or the seed."

So Tally read, and Dr. Mineral listened, and the founding age came up out of the dark around them.

It had not been a triumph. That was the first thing, and it surprised Tally, who had grown up on tales of the glorious laboratory beneath the world. The laboratory had begun in *grief.* In the founding age the world above had been losing things — meadows going under stone, rivers going foul, whole green kinds of life winking out one after another faster than anyone could mourn them. And a few small creatures, no one important, no one a story would have chosen, had not been able to bear it. So they had begun, quietly, to carry things down into the dark where the cold could not reach them and the world above could not pave them over. A feather. A flower. A drop of a drying sea. A seed.

They had not called it a laboratory. They had not called it anything. The oldest journal Tally could find called it, simply, *the keeping.*

"Here," she breathed. She had found a page so old the ink had gone to autumn-brown, written in a round careful hand. She held it to the lamp and read it slowly, and the warm dim Archives held still to listen.

It told of the seed. Of how it had come down in the founding cold — not found in one bright moment by one clever creature, but *arrived*, the journal said, after a long travelling. It had been kept alive through more winters than the writer could count before it ever reached the keeping, passed from paw to paw, from those who could no longer carry it to those who could, across a stretch of years so long that the writer themselves did not know where it had begun. *We did not save it,* the old hand had written. *We are only the latest to carry it, and we pray not the last. There are too many to name in this. We have written, instead, that it was kept by love, which is the truest name we have for so many hands.*

For a while no one in the reading-cell spoke. Tally found that her eyes had gone hot.

"Too many to name," said Dr. Mineral, very quietly. "They weren't hiding anyone. They were *bowing* to them. They knew exactly what they had been given, and they knew it was bigger than any list, and they were honest enough not to pretend otherwise." He looked at the brown old page with something like love. "The empty place isn't a wound, Tally. It's a window. The founders left it open on purpose, so we would always have to look through it and remember that the best thing we have was given by more hands than we will ever count."

"Then why does it feel like a wound?" Tally asked. It was a real question, not an argument. "Why is the whole laboratory frightened tonight, if it was meant kindly?"

Dr. Mineral had no answer for that yet, and he was honest enough, himself, to say so.

It was Vellum who, lifting his great head, gave them the lead that would lead them wrong.

"There is a name," the old tortoise said slowly. "I had near forgotten it. The young ones used to be told it, long ago, before even my time — a tale, more than a record. *Old Hazel,* they called her. She who carried the seed down into the keeping. There are pages for her, deeper in, illuminated, painted — the Archives loved her. If any single creature is remembered as the seed's, it is Hazel." He looked at the empty-windowed page, and then at the squirrel, torn between his founders' honest *too many to name* and the simpler comfort of a single beloved face. "Perhaps the founders were humble past all use. Perhaps there *was* a one, after all, and her name is sitting in these very Archives waiting to be carried back to the empty place at last."

Tally was already on her feet, bright-eyed. A name. A face. Someone to thank.

Dr. Mineral rose more slowly. He wanted it to be true — he was not made of stone; he wanted, as much as any of them, to put a beloved name in the window and end the long fear in the halls.

Which was exactly why he did not trust how badly he wanted it.

"Show us Old Hazel," he said. "Let's meet her properly. And let's find out whether she is the answer — " he touched the acorn at his cheek " — or only the answer we'd like."
