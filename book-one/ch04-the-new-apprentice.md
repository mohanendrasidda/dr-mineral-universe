# Chapter Four — The New Apprentice

*Book I — Dr. Mineral and the Unsigned Discovery · draft v1*
*Beat ② — "every contribution matters" lived, not lectured. Introduces Tally and the Contribution Registry; the lab's mission shown through how it remembers. Answers: who she is, what the Registry is, what she found. Raises: mistake, or something real? No tech named.*

---

Her name was Tally, and she had been a researcher of our laboratory for exactly one morning.

Dr. Mineral learned this slowly, the way he learned everything, sitting with her a little apart from the others while the great hall murmured and waited. He had asked the crowd, gently, to give the child room to breathe, and they had, because he had asked. Now there was just the two of them on a low bench of worn wood, and the young magpie's shaking had eased to a tremor, and she was telling him, in a rush and then in fits, how she had come to find a hole in the bottom of the world.

She had arrived that very dawn, down the long stair for the first time, her heart so loud she had been sure the lamps could hear it. She was to apprentice in the Contribution Registry. She had wanted it more than she had ever wanted anything. Above ground, she said — and here she ducked her head, because it sounded foolish — above ground, magpies were laughed at. Thieves, people called them. Birds who took bright things and hid them and forgot them. But that was the very reason she had loved the idea of the Registry. A place whose whole work was the opposite of forgetting.

Dr. Mineral did not laugh. He said, "Tell me about your morning," and he meant it, and she saw that he meant it, and that was when she began to trust him.

The Contribution Registry, she told him — though of course he knew it, had known it for forty years, but he let her tell it, because a thing is yours in a new way once you have said it out loud — the Registry was the quietest of all the halls. No benches, no arguments, no diagrams carried like sails. Only the long dim aisles, and the soft endless ticking, and the machines.

She did not know how to describe the machines. Neither, in truth, did he, though he had stood among them a thousand times. They filled the Registry from its floor to its lost ceiling, rank on rank, brass and glass and dark wood, turning very slowly, always turning, with a sound like a thousand patient clocks that were not measuring time but something else. Each one held, somehow — she did not understand how, and Vellum had told her that not understanding how was a perfectly good place to begin — each one held the memory of a contribution. Not the thing itself. The *fact* of it. That on such a day, in such a hall, someone had asked a question, or mended a flaw, or noticed what no one else had noticed, and that it had mattered, and that they had been the one. The smallest correction by the smallest mouse turned its own small wheel somewhere in that vast dim hall, and the wheel would go on turning, faithfully, long after the mouse was gone, so that no contribution — not one, not ever — would go the way magpies were said to make bright things go. Lost. Hidden. Forgotten.

That was the whole promise of the place. That was the thing our laboratory had been built, across all its centuries, to learn how to keep: that what you give can be remembered, and carried, and handed on, so that those who come after stand on it without even knowing it is there.

Tally's first task had been the smallest one they had. They always were, for a first morning. Vellum had set her to *walking the rows* — only walking, looking, learning the feel of the place — and, if she liked, choosing one wheel, any wheel, and following its thread back to read the contribution it kept. A way to fall in love with the work. Most apprentices chose something grand. The discovery of the warm river. The first green light.

Tally, being Tally, had wanted to know what the oldest one remembered.

So she had gone to the deepest aisle, the dim far back where the machines were dark with age and the dust lay soft, and she had found the wheel that turned slowest of all — so slow you had to hold your breath and watch a long time to be sure it moved — because the slowest wheel, she reasoned, must be the oldest thread, the first thing our laboratory had ever thought worth keeping.

And she had followed its thread to read the name of who had given it.

And there was no name.

"Not a worn-away name," she said to Dr. Mineral, and her small voice had gone very steady now, the way a voice does when it has decided to be brave. "Not a lost card or a faded one. I've read the worn ones — Keeper Vellum showed me how, you hold them to the lamp. This isn't worn. There's a place where the name goes. And the place is *empty.* It was made empty. As if no one was ever there to put one." She looked up at him, this child who had been a researcher for one morning, and asked him the question that had emptied the Hall. "How can the very first thing we ever decided to remember — be the one thing we never knew who to thank?"

Dr. Mineral was quiet for a long moment.

He was thinking that in forty years he had never gone to the deepest aisle and looked at the slowest wheel. None of them had. You don't, with the foundation. You stand on it.

"What does it keep?" he asked at last. "The slowest wheel. What is the contribution?"

"That's the part no one will say." Tally's feathers prickled. "When I told Keeper Vellum, he went so still. And then he didn't take me to the Registry. He brought me here, and he sent for *you.* Because the thread doesn't point at a thing in the Registry at all." She swallowed. "It points down. To the Crystal Vault. To whatever it is they keep at the very bottom, that the whole laboratory grows from."
