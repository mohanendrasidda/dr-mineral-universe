# Chapter Seven — Why It Matters

*Book I — Dr. Mineral and the Unsigned Discovery · draft v1*
*Beat ④ — stakes without dystopia. The fear spreads gently; trust frays; Dr. Mineral sees the true stakes — not a name to recover but a trust to keep. No villain. No tech named.*

---

The trouble with a question is that it does not stay where you put it.

Dr. Mineral had meant to keep the matter small and quiet — a few of them, the Registry, the slow patient work he loved. But news in our laboratory traveled the way warmth travels through water, evenly and everywhere at once, and by evening of that first day there was not a hall so deep or a researcher so busy that the words had not reached them. *The seed has no name. The heart of the laboratory was given by no one we can thank. The promise has a hole in it.*

He felt the change before he understood it. He was crossing the high gallery toward the Registry, late, when he passed a bench where an old mole had worked at the same problem of soil-water for as long as anyone could remember — a small problem, an endless one, the kind that never makes a famous wheel turn but quietly keeps a thousand green things alive. The mole was not working. He was sitting with his blunt paws still on the bench, looking at nothing.

"It's a fine problem you keep," Dr. Mineral said, sitting down beside him, because he never passed a still creature without stopping.

"Is it," said the mole. Not unkindly. Just tired. "I used to think so." He turned his soft face toward the squirrel. "I've given this place sixty years, Doctor. Little things. Nothing anyone would put in the Vault. I never minded. I always thought — well. I thought the wheel was turning. I thought that somewhere down in the Registry there was a small dark wheel with my sixty years on it, and that it would go on turning after me, and some apprentice four hundred winters from now might follow its thread and learn that an old mole once cared very much about water in soil." He looked back at his still paws. "And now I find the *seed* has no wheel. The seed. And I think — if they could lose that, Doctor. If they could lose the very biggest one." He did not finish. He did not have to.

Dr. Mineral sat with him a long while and did not offer him an easy answer, because he had just spent a morning refusing easy answers and he was not going to hand one to a friend now. He only said, at last, "You're right that it's the biggest one. That's exactly why I won't let it lie." And he sat a little longer, until the mole picked up his small tool again — not because he believed, yet, but because the squirrel had stayed.

But the mole was not the only one.

All through the deep halls, that evening, the same small stillness was spreading. Not panic. Our laboratory did not do panic. Something quieter and more dangerous: a creeping doubt, creature by creature, that the thing they had all been standing on was not as solid as they had trusted. You give your work to a place because you believe the place will keep it. Take that belief away — even a little, even at the edges — and the giving gets harder. A young otter put off recording her night's small finding until tomorrow, *what's the hurry, what's the point.* A family of mice argued, for the first time anyone could recall, about whose name should go on a thing they had always simply done together. It was nothing. It was everything. It was the first thin crack of frost across a pond that had frozen solid for a thousand years.

Dr. Mineral understood it now, fully, and the understanding sat heavy in him.

He had thought, that morning, that he was solving a mystery — a puzzle, a cold case, a name fallen through a floor. He saw now that he was not. The name was almost the smallest part. The thing that had gone wrong was not in the past. It was happening, right now, all around him, in the still paws and the put-off recordings and the first unkind argument among the mice — the slow quiet unmaking of the one thing our laboratory was actually made of, which was not stone, or machines, or even knowledge. It was *trust.* The plain belief that what you give will be held, and honored, and handed on. Every wheel in the Registry was only brass and glass. The promise was the real machine, and it ran on trust the way the water-rooms ran on water, and trust, he was learning, could drain.

He found Tally in the Registry, where she had refused to leave, sitting in the dim by the slowest wheel with her chin on the rim of it, watching it not-quite-turn.

"They're frightened," she said, without looking up. She had heard it too, all evening, the change in the halls. "Up above they'd say it serves us right. The clever underground place that thought it could keep every little thing forever." Her voice was small. "Maybe it was always going to crack. Maybe you can't really keep a promise that big."

Dr. Mineral sat down beside her, the way he had sat beside the mole, the way he had sat beside the frightened bird under the hedge that morning a hundred years ago.

"When I was young," he said, "younger than you, I found a bird with a broken wing. I couldn't mend it. I have never been able to mend a wing in my life." Tally looked up. "But I could sit with it until it stopped being afraid. And do you know — that didn't fix the wing. But it was not nothing. It was the opposite of nothing." He nodded at the slow wheel. "We may not be able to give the seed its name back. I don't know yet. But the fear in these halls tonight isn't really about a name four centuries gone. It's every creature here asking, *will I be remembered?* And that question we *can* answer. Not by finding one old name." He stood, and held out a paw to her. "By showing them, with everything we've got, that we would rather turn this whole laboratory upside down than let one contribution go unthanked. They need to see us *care* that much. Caring that much, out loud, where everyone can see — that's how trust comes back."

Tally took his paw and stood.

"Then we'd better start caring loudly," she said. "Where do we read an empty place?"
