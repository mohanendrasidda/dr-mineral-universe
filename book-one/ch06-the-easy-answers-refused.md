# Chapter Six — The Easy Answers, Refused

*Book I — Dr. Mineral and the Unsigned Discovery · draft v1*
*Beat ③ → ④ — character and method established. The laboratory offers easy ways to make the trouble disappear; Dr. Mineral refuses each and chooses to ask. Answers: what they will not do. Raises: how do you investigate a question centuries cold? No tech named.*

---

By the time they came back up the old blue stair, the Central Hall had found its voice again, and the voice was full of solutions.

This is what frightened creatures do. They had stood in silence while the trouble was only a silence; but now the trouble had a shape — *the seed has no name* — and a thing with a shape can be argued with, and arguing felt so much better than fear that the whole Hall had thrown itself into it gladly. Dr. Mineral came off the last step into a warm storm of helpfulness, and he stood at the edge of it a while, as he liked to do, and listened before he spoke.

A heron from the Observatory had the first easy answer, and it was the gentlest. "Then we give it one," she said. "A name. Surely among all our records there is a founder, an elder, someone worthy — we choose the best of them, and we write it in, and the place is no longer empty. The seed is honored. We can all sleep."

A murmur of relief went round. It was such a kind idea. Dr. Mineral let it travel all the way around the Hall before he answered it, because he wanted them to hear it land.

"And if we choose wrong?" he said. "If we write in a great name, a name we admire, and it was not theirs — then we have not filled the empty place. We have *covered* it. We have taken the one contribution we failed to remember, and we have given the credit to someone who did not earn it, and called the matter closed." He shook his head, slowly. "That is not a name. That is a lid."

The heron looked down, but she was not hurt; she had wanted to be argued out of it. That was why she had said it aloud.

A stoat from the Innovation Workshop had the next answer, and it was not gentle, and it was the one Dr. Mineral had been dreading, because he could see how many heads nodded at it. "There is a name we would not be guessing," the stoat said. He did not look at Dr. Mineral as he said it. He looked, pointedly, at everyone else. "Our laboratory had a founder. The first Chief Researcher. The one whose work all of this — " he spread his small dark paws at the glowing galleries " — descends from. If the seed is our heart, and he built the body around it, then is it truly wrong to say the credit is, in a sense, already his?"

It went very quiet.

They were not looking at the stoat now. They were looking at Dr. Mineral.

He understood. Of course he understood. He was old, and he was loved, and he was the nearest thing the laboratory had to a beginning that anyone alive could remember. It would be so easy. He had only to be a little silent, a little modest, to murmur *oh, surely not me* in the particular way that means *yes*, and the empty place would close around his own name, and the Hall would weep and cheer and feel, for a while, that the world was whole again. It would cost him nothing but the truth.

"I did not save the seed," he said.

The words were plain and quiet and they shut the idea like a book.

"I have never grown a thing in my life that this seed did not give me the air to grow it in. I came *after.* All of us came after. Every one of us in this Hall, down to the youngest — " he did not look at Tally, but the whole Hall did " — is alive because of a gift we did not make and have not yet repaid. To take its credit because I happen to be old, because it would comfort us, because the empty place hurts to look at — " He stopped. When he went on his voice had gone very gentle, which was how they all knew he meant it most. "My friends. We are the laboratory that was built so that no contribution would ever be lost. And the first thing we want to do, the very first morning we discover one *has* been, is to lose it again on purpose, more comfortably, with a nicer name on it. No. I would rather have an empty place I am ashamed of than a full one I have to lie to."

No one spoke. But something in the Hall changed — the shame turned, very slightly, from fear into resolve, the way it does in a good crowd when someone says the hard true thing out loud.

"Then what?" said the stoat, and now he did look at Dr. Mineral, and there was no fight in it, only the same tiredness they all felt. "It was centuries ago. There is no one to question. The thread points at nothing. How do you find an answer that old and that cold? Where would you even begin?"

Dr. Mineral reached up and touched the acorn at his cheek, the way he did when he was thinking, the dark acorn that had rolled uphill that morning to bring him here.

"The same way we find every answer," he said. "We don't go looking for the whole truth. No one of us is big enough to hold it. We go looking for one small true thing. And then another. And we trust that they will add up, because they always have." He looked round at them all — the heron, the stoat, the old tortoise, the trembling magpie who was not trembling now. "That is the only thing this laboratory has ever known how to do. A thousand small noticings, laid side by side, until they make a shape no single one of us could have seen alone." He almost smiled. "We were built for exactly this. We only forgot, because we have never had to use it on ourselves."

"Begin where?" said Vellum, very low.

"Where the child began," said Dr. Mineral. "At the slowest wheel. We are going back into the Registry — and this time, we are going to read the empty place itself."
